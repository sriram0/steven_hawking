Stephen Hawking

Stephen William Hawking was an English theoretical physicist and cosmologist. He was born on the 8th of January, 1942 and died on the 14th of March, 2018. He is known for his work on singularities and his discovery of Hawking Radiation, so far the only confirmed way that a black hole can meet its demise.

Just before his 21st birthday, Hawking was diagnosed with Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis which causes his nuerons involved with muscle movement to degrade slowly. He was told that he wouldn't live more than two years but that didn't stop him. Eventually, he required a wheel chair and in 1985 he was diagnosed with severe pneumonia and underwent tracheotomy which cost him his voice. He communicated using an artificial voice and selected words by moving the few functional muscles that were left.

Despite his failing muscles, in 1973, Hawking decided to combine general relativity with quantum mechanics, two things that by definition aren't supposed to overlap, but can due to the concentration of over a star's worth of mass in an infinitely small point at the centers of black holes. He concluded that black holes slowly radiate particles over time before ending with an explosion. The way this what's now known as Hawking Radiation works is as follows:

  1. At the event horizon, the energy from the black hole creates a pair of matter and antimatter particles
  2. Usually, the particles will just merge and turn back into energy but sometimes, the black hole swallows one of the particles which means that the other particles can't merge
  3. It is then radiated away, carrying with it a bit of the black hole's mass
Because taking subatomic quantities of mass from something with atleast the mass of a few suns is so slow, it takes a long time for a black hole to dissipate. Eons is an understatement. It would take 1.15975*1067 years for a black hole with the Sun's mass to dissipate. That's about twelve thousand vigintillion. The fact that he was able to do all this with severe and uncurable ALS shows determination and persistence. He survived a miraculous 55 years with the disease before succumbing on the 14th of March, 2018; only 10% of ALS patients survive 10 years after being diagnosed with it.
